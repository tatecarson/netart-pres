Nam June Paik

Communication art

In many ways Paik was a precursor to internet art. Many of his works with tv and satellite foreshadowed the communication possibilities of the internet.

Nam June Paik "Participation TV" from Bright Eye Cinema on Vimeo.

"Participation TV" was built by Nam June Paik in 1969. Two microphones control patterns on a television set. Paik says The Video Synthesizer was "actually somehow the beginning of the Internet...Because you were able to create media content for yourself, like you can do on the Internet now. It is very important to make media yourself. Now with the PC and the Internet we have a better chance of doing something." Paik also coined the term "information superhighway" which became used to describe the possibilities of the internet.
''Wrap Around the World,'' a 90-minute live television broadcast featuring performances and other events produced simultaneously from 10 countries in Europe and Asia, was broadcast 9/10/1988. It was a State-of-the-art transmission with new video effects. This clip features David Bowie and Performance artists La La La Human Steps in a special rendition of his song "Look Back in Anger" with a special introduction by Al Franken and Dr. Mobius - an alien from another planet (Tom Davis). The whole event was held together by Nam June Paik's video graphics, which included one of his stacked television sculptures. Posted in recognition of the loss of one of the great performers of the last 40 years spanning Glam Rock, Jazz, Blues, Rock-n-roll and Pop. He never did what was expected and always pushed the envelope. David Bowie died 1/11/16 and will be missed.

Douglas Davis

The World's First Collaborative Sentence

considered one of the first works of internet art. Created in 1994 for an exhibition in an art gallery. The work was related to Davis's exhibition also being shown called InterActions (1967-1981). Davis says he wanted to focus on the keyboard as a means of interaction that differentiated it from other media such as the TV. About it he says: "This means a lot more intense and personal link could occur between me and the audience. Davis also did many works involving live satellite performances similar to Paik's.
Ken Goldberg
The Telegarden (1995-2004) : art installation that allows web users to view and interact with a remote garden filled with living plants. Members can plant, water, and monitor the progress of seedlings via the tender movements of an industrial robot arm.
Ken Goldberg is the William S. Floyd Distinguished Chair in Engineering at UC Berkeley and an award-winning roboticist, filmmaker, artist and popular public speaker on AI and robotics. Ken trains the next generation of researchers and entrepreneurs in his research lab at UC Berkeley; he has published over 300 papers, 3 books, and holds 9 US Patents. Ken’s artwork has been featured in 70 art exhibits including the 2000 Whitney Biennial. He is a pioneer in technology and artistic visual expression, bridging the “two cultures” of art and science. With unique skills in communication and creative problem solving, invention, and thinking on the edge, Ken has presented over 600 invited lectures at events around the world.
Lia Austrian artist LIA is considered one of the pioneers of software and net art and has been producing works since 1995. Her practice spans across video, performance, software, installations, sculpture, projections and digital applications.
“The pieces on www.re-move.org are an attempt to articulate mathematical and natural principles below a visual surface in an enjoyable and sometimes playful way. People should be able to get their own ideas about the pieces and involve themselves without having any preconception of how to watch or how to “use” the pieces – that’s why there are no instructions or any guidelines. Depending of the used formulas and also depending on who is watching/playing around, the results of the created images and/or sounds will always be different – in that sense, the user can also be seen as an additional random-factor in the code which can help to create a more or less “organic” result.”
JODI Internet provocateurs JODI pioneered Web art in the mid-1990s. Based in The Netherlands, JODI (Joan Heemskerk and Dirk Paesmans) were among the first artists to investigate and subvert conventions of the Internet, computer programs, and video and computer games. Radically disrupting the very language of these systems, including interfaces, commands, errors and code, JODI stages extreme digital interventions that destabilize the relationship between computer technology and its users.

1998 -- The web browser is an organ of perception through which we 'see' the web. It filters and organizes a huge mass of structured information that spans continents, is constantly growing, reorganizing itself, shifting its appearance, evolving. The Shredder presents this global structure as a chaotic, irrational, raucous "action painting". By altering the HTML code before the browser reads it, the Shredder appropriates the data of the web, transforming it into a parallel web. Content become abstraction. Text becomes graphics. Information becomes art.
1999 -- Riot is an alternative, “cross-content” Web browser. Like it's real-world namesake, Riot disrupts the accepted rules of property and exposes the fragility of territorial boundaries. Inspired by the clashing classes and ideologies of New York's lower east side, Riot is a software coded "melting pot", a blender that mixes web pages from separate domains into one browser window.

2020 - The ability to understand and share other people’s feelings and problems is known as empathy. On the web page empathi.cc, artist Raphaël Bastide created a graphic element which can be moved and can mimic visual attributes from its neighbours. The style transfer between the page’s elements can be considered as a minimal version of empathy, where the distance between objects triggers visual mutations. Consequently, the main graphic element gets affected by an accumulating memory of style properties depending on its path.
